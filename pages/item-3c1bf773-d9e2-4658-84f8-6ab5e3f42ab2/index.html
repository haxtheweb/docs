5. Maximize student attention by keeping the video short

Student-Centered Learning Objectives

Before we can think about your video's suitability to your instructional goals for students, we must have these instructional goals clearly in view. Have you articulated your learning objectives in terms of how the student will demonstrate attainment of them? What evidence of learning will satisfy you that an adequate kind and level of learning has taken place? Bear in mind that the higher-level objectives we often expect can only be achieved once lower-level objectives have been attained, like rungs on a ladder.

A fuller discussion of properly written learning objectives is beyond the scope of this exhibit. See Bloom’s Taxonomy and writing learning objectives for guidance.

Learning Objectives-Strategy Match

Ensure that a video lecture is the best instructional strategy for your objectives. Here are just a few examples of what lecture video does and does not do well instructionally:

What Lecture Video Does Well

  • Convey facts and foundational knowledge
  • Tour students through a rich range of visuals with commentary
  • Provide a careful clarification of often-misunderstood topics or key ideas

  • Teach how to evaluate or create content

Present Objectives Early

Early in the video, make the connection between the relevant learning objectives and the video explicit. Bring your objectives into the video itself, at least within the first minute.

Track Student Usage (if possible)

This guideline is entirely dependent on the data capture capabilities of the technology where your video resides, and so may not always be possible. But, if available, this data is vital for "closing the instructional loop" and shedding light on student use of your video.

  • How many students are watching the video is its entirety?
  • Do they watch it all at once, or in multiple sessions?
  • What buttons do they click in the video player? Do they rewind to review?

The Office of Digital Learning may be able to help with tracking student usage of your video.

Assess Student Comprehension

This is an especially important consideration, often missed. Watching a video is an essentially passive activity. Did they understand your explanations? Did they connect concepts and relationships correctly? Are they clear on the major points, or did they "major on the minors"?Frankly, without some mechanism to find out, you don't know and neither do they. What can you do?

  • At selected points in the video, interject a "check your understanding" question before going further. Applications like H5P allow you to author and associate a wide range of question types with your video.
  • Use an ungraded Canvas quiz activity immediately following the video.
  • Use a poll to get the pulse of the class and then provide reaction/clarification.

Whatever assessment method you use, be sure that it aligns with the learning objectives you identified previously.

Relate to Class Discussion (Optional)

If discussion is important in your course, what role does video play in that discussion? The video should be located together with the associated discussion forum.

Consider Managed Release of Content

Determine how you will make your video lectures available to students:

  • All at once?
  • Weekly?
  • Unit by unit?
  • Only upon completion of other content?

A managed release of content is often beneficial to students, as it conveys a sense of course pace and keeps them from feeling overwhelmed. If you do not want to release all content at once, consider making an additional lesson beyond the current lesson available to allow your faster students to work ahead at least a bit.

Integrateâ€‹ Video into the Course

Consider how the video will be integrated with the rest of the course components. Your lecture video is only one activity in a larger learning sequence, which has implications for where you place your video links.

Will the links be listed sequentially? Will they be centralized, distributed throughout the course, or both? Will other content be associated with the video, such as a note-taking template, additional readings, or an assignment?

6. Use a conversational style (Mayer's personalization principle)

Shorter is Better

Across studies, research consistently shows that shorter duration associates with higher completion and improved retention. Specifically, somewhere between 6-12 minutes is ideal , with 15 minutes being the maximum.

Organize Content into Narrow, Specific Topics

When chunking down a longer video, use narrower, more specific topics. For example, instead of one video named "Ancient Rome", break the video down into a series of three: "Why Did Rome Rise?", "Tracing Roman Design Features", and "Rome's Relationship to Nature."

Make the Viewing Sequence Clear

When multiple videos are best viewed in a particular sequence, make that sequence clear through sequential numbering of the video titles themselves.

Using our Rome example as the third lesson in a course, those become "3.1 Why Did Rome Rise?", "3.2 Tracing Roman Design Features", and "3.3 Rome's Relationship to Nature."
