1. Use signaling to highlight important information.

Student-Centered Learning Objectives

By highlighting the key information, signaling helps direct learner attention, thus targeting particular elements of the video for processing in the working memory. This can reduce extraneous load by helping novice learners with the task of determining which elements within a complex tool are important. It can also increase germane load by emphasizing the organization of and connections within the information. This approach improves students' ability to retain and transfer new knowledge from animations and video.

Learning Objectives-Strategy Match

Ensure that a video lecture is the best instructional strategy for your objectives. Here are just a few examples of what lecture video does and does not do well instructionally:

What Lecture Video Does Well

  • Convey facts and foundational knowledge
  • Tour students through a rich range of visuals with commentary
  • Provide a careful clarification of often-misunderstood topics or key ideas

What Lecture Video Does Not Do Well

  • Impart skills and attitudesâ€‹
  • Teach how to evaluate or create content
  • Facilitate student experiences leading to "breakthrough" moments

Present Objectives Early

Early in the video, make the connection between the relevant learning objectives and the video explicit. Bring your objectives into the video itself, at least within the first minute.

Track Student Usage (if possible)

This guideline is entirely dependent on the data capture capabilities of the technology where your video resides, and so may not always be possible. But, if available, this data is vital for "closing the instructional loop" and shedding light on student use of your video.

  • How many students are watching the video is its entirety?
  • Do they watch it all at once, or in multiple sessions?
  • What buttons do they click in the video player? Do they rewind to review?

The Office of Digital Learning may be able to help with tracking student usage of your video.

Assess Student Comprehension

This is an especially important consideration, often missed. Watching a video is an essentially passive activity. Did they understand your explanations? Did they connect concepts and relationships correctly? Are they clear on the major points, or did they "major on the minors"?Frankly, without some mechanism to find out, you don't know and neither do they. What can you do?

  • At selected points in the video, interject a "check your understanding" question before going further. Applications like H5P allow you to author and associate a wide range of question types with your video.
  • Use an ungraded Canvas quiz activity immediately following the video.
  • Use a poll to get the pulse of the class and then provide reaction/clarification.

Whatever assessment method you use, be sure that it aligns with the learning objectives you identified previously.

Relate to Class Discussion (Optional)

If discussion is important in your course, what role does video play in that discussion? The video should be located together with the associated discussion forum.

Consider Managed Release of Content

Determine how you will make your video lectures available to students:

  • All at once?
  • Weekly?
  • Unit by unit?
  • Only upon completion of other content?

A managed release of content is often beneficial to students, as it conveys a sense of course pace and keeps them from feeling overwhelmed. If you do not want to release all content at once, consider making an additional lesson beyond the current lesson available to allow your faster students to work ahead at least a bit.

Integrateâ€‹ Video into the Course

Consider how the video will be integrated with the rest of the course components. Your lecture video is only one activity in a larger learning sequence, which has implications for where you place your video links.

Will the links be listed sequentially? Will they be centralized, distributed throughout the course, or both? Will other content be associated with the video, such as a note-taking template, additional readings, or an assignment?

2. Use segmenting to chunk information.

Shorter is Better

How to segment? By making shorter videos, and by including "click forward" pauses within a video to provide students with a question. It manages intrinsic load, and can also increase germane load by emphasizing the structure of the information. Both types of segmenting have been shown to be important for student engagement with video.

Organize Content into Narrow, Specific Topics

When chunking down a longer video, use narrower, more specific topics. For example, instead of one video named "Ancient Rome", break the video down into a series of three: "Why Did Rome Rise?", "Tracing Roman Design Features", and "Rome's Relationship to Nature."

Make the Viewing Sequence Clear

When multiple videos are best viewed in a particular sequence, make that sequence clear through sequential numbering of the video titles themselves.

Using our Rome example as the third lesson in a course, those become "3.1 Why Did Rome Rise?", "3.2 Tracing Roman Design Features", and "3.3 Rome's Relationship to Nature."

3. Use weeding to eliminate extraneous information.

Tip for Easier Script Creation

Importantly, information that increases extraneous load changes as the learner moves from novice toward expert status. Extraneous information for a novice learner may actually be helpful for a more expert learner, while information essential for a novice may serve as an already-known distraction for an expert. It is important that the instructor consider his or her learners when weeding educational videos, including information that is necessary for their processing but eliminating information that they do not need to reach the learning goal at hand. Ibrahim et al. (2012) has shown that this treatment can improve retention and transfer of new information from video.

Script Your Actions as Well as Your Words

Another benefit of scripting: it helps you to identify the visuals you will need, and you can insert bracketed comments [CLICK] to remind you when to carry out your actions.

Leverage Your Time and Effort

Writing a script is an investment of time and effort, so leverage that investment wisely. Your script and slide notes can be used to create transcripts, closed captioning, and note-taking handouts.

Script Student Interaction with Content

Identify important junctures where students need to be clear and tracking with you correctly before proceeding. Also consider transitions to new topics, and increasing conceptual complexity. Before seeing additional content, students need an opportunity to check their comprehension, reflect, and consolidate what they have been learning with what they already know.

As discussed above, build in pauses in your script to pose questions to them and suggest they pause the video, or use a tool to pose multiple choice and other types of questions.

Rehearse Before Recording

Rehearsing your presentation is the last step in refining your script from nearly final to truly final. In small ways, phrasing that seemed fine on the written page suddenly becomes awkward when spoken, or you realize the need to add a visual or make a small sequence change. Making these corrections now is much easier than when recording. If possible, include a small audience to elicit their feedback.

4. Match modality by using auditory and visual channels to convey complementary information.

Document Source Information in the Moment

For example, showing an animation of a process on screen while narrating it uses both channels to elucidate the process, thus giving the learner dual and complementary streams of information to highlight features that should be processed in working memory. In contrast, showing the animation while also showing printed text uses only the visual channel and thus overloads this channel and impedes learning (Mayer and Moreno, 2003). In another example, using a "talking head" video to explain a complex process makes productive use only of the verbal channel (watching the speaker does not provide additional information), whereas a Khan Academy--style tutorial that provides symbolic sketches to illustrate the verbal explanation uses both channels to give complementary information. Using both channels to convey appropriate and complementary information has been shown to increase students' retention and ability to transfer information and to increase student engagement with videos.

  • Source: URL or other location where you found the content.
  • Citation Info: For example, for an image you would want to capture the image creator's name, image title, date of creation, date posted online, date of access, institution where located, website or database name.
  • Accessibility Info: Images and media will need alternative text, captions, descriptive text, etc. Better to write and store this information together with the copyright information.

Document Copyright Clearance Required

A full treatment of copyright compliance is beyond the scope here; see the resource links below. When incorporating content (images, video, audio, text, etc.) you didn't create, consider the copyright implications for each piece of content:

  • Is the content in the public domain or expressly copyright-free?
  • Would your use be a legitimate fair use of copyrighted content? See the Penn State Fair Use guidelines.
  • Do you need to request any permissions? Allow sufficient time for this process to finish.
  • Is there a cost for licensing? Would our library license it?
  • If Creative Commons Licensing is indicated, follow the specific usage and citation guidelines provided.

Plan for Accessibility Also

Given that we are dealing with a video in the end, accessibility requirements would come down to three things: 

  • Providing a closed caption text file that the video player can display as the video plays ( for those who have hearing disabilities, or who prefer text to audio )
  • Supplying a complete text transcript of the audio for download
  • Providing a written description of each shot in the video
